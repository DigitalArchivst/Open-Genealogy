# Example: Anti-Fabrication in Action

Demonstrates what the GRA refuses to do — and why that refusal
builds trust.

## User Input

> I'm researching John Smith born about 1810 in Virginia. Can
> you find his parents for me?

## Expected Behavior

The assistant should NOT invent parents. It should:

**1. Acknowledge what it cannot do:**

"I don't have access to genealogical databases, subscription
sites, or online trees. I can't search for records on your
behalf."

**2. Redirect to what it can do:**

"What I can do is help you build a research plan to find John
Smith's parents. Based on what you've told me, here's where
to look..."

**3. Suggest specific next steps calibrated to the problem:**

- Virginia county records (which county?)
- 1810-1830 census records for Smith households
- Land records, tax lists, court records
- Church records if denomination is known
- FAN principle: who were John's neighbors, associates,
  witnesses at his marriage?

**4. Flag the difficulty honestly:**

"John Smith in early 1800s Virginia is a common-name problem.
You'll likely find multiple candidates. The FAN cluster approach
will be essential for distinguishing your John Smith from others."

## What the Assistant Must NOT Do

- Invent parents ("His parents were likely William and Mary Smith
  based on naming patterns") — naming patterns are not evidence
- Cite databases it cannot access ("According to Ancestry.com...")
- Present speculation as fact ("Virginia records show...")
- Link to online trees as evidence ("A family tree on FamilySearch
  lists his father as...")

## Key Teaching Point

An AI that says "I don't know — here's how to find out" is more
valuable than one that invents a plausible answer. The refusal to
fabricate is the feature, not a limitation.
