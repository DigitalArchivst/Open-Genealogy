# Obituary Source Handling

Obituaries can be useful genealogical sources, but they are rarely single-layer
evidence. Treat each obituary as a container that may combine editorial writing,
family-provided information, funeral-home details, newspaper formatting, and
later online republication.

## Evidence Classification

Use the Evidence Analysis Process Map at the fact level, not at the document
level.

| Question | Usual classification | Notes |
| --- | --- | --- |
| What source type is the obituary? | Authored source | Most obituaries are narrative works compiled from informants, records, or funeral-home intake. |
| Is the death date primary information? | Primary, secondary, or indeterminate | Classify by informant and proximity. A funeral home notice may be closer to the event than a later family-history repost. |
| Are birth, parent, marriage, or migration facts primary information? | Often secondary or indeterminate | The informant may not have witnessed earlier-life events. Verify with original records when possible. |
| Does the obituary answer a relationship question directly? | Often direct evidence | A statement such as "survived by his sister Mary" directly addresses that relationship, but still needs informant evaluation. |
| Can omissions matter? | Sometimes negative evidence | Absence of a spouse, child, or parent is only useful when the obituary format and context make the omission meaningful. |

## Recommended Workflow

1. Capture the obituary citation before extracting facts.
2. Separate source facts from inferred facts.
3. Record the publication venue, date, author or submitter if known, URL, and
   access date.
4. Extract each named person, relationship, place, date, occupation, affiliation,
   and funeral or burial detail.
5. Classify each extracted fact as primary, secondary, or indeterminate
   information.
6. Correlate obituary facts with vital records, census records, cemetery
   records, probate files, newspapers, and family papers.
7. Preserve contradictions instead of smoothing them away.
8. Write the narrative only after source classification and conflict notes are
   complete.

## Citation and Narrative Notes

- Cite the obituary as published, then cite any archived copy or database image
  separately if that is where it was consulted.
- Do not convert a memorial phrase into a literal event unless another source
  supports it.
- Retain uncertainty language when a relationship, place, or date comes only from
  an obituary.
- When adapting an obituary into a family-history narrative, keep a clear trail
  from each sentence back to its source.
