## NAMES FOUND IN HISTORY
### Many of His Descendants Have Left Their Marks on Time

## STATUE UNVEILED THIS WEEK
### Long List of Great-Grandchildren and Other Descendants to Attend Thursday's Ceremony

**BY WILLIAM E. CURTIS**
*Written for The Star and the Chicago Record Herald*

If all the descendants of John Witherspoon come to see his monument unveiled May 20, at the conjunction of Connecticut avenue, 18th and N streets, in this city, the accommodations for spectators will have to be larger than usual. The list is long, and contains the names of many men and women who have been prominent in peace and war, in politics, diplomacy, education, in the pulpit, at the bar, and in the Congress of the United States. John Cabell Breckinridge of Kentucky, Vice President of the United States from 1856 to 1860, Clifton R. Breckinridge of Arkansas, who served with distinction in the House of Representatives, and was ambassador to Russia during the Cleveland administration, and many others of the great tribe of Breckinridge call John Witherspoon grandfather; Mrs. John Sharp Williams, wife of the late leader of the democratic minority of the House of Representatives, and senator-elect from the state of Mississippi; Mrs. Charles Francis Adams of Boston, Mme. Godoy, wife of a former minister from Chile to the United States; Gen. Wotherspoon of the United States Army, who spells his name differently; Rev. Dr. Jere Witherspoon of Richmond, one of the most prominent and eloquent divines in the south; several officers of the army and navy, and the wives of others, appear in a list of 129 descendants, which has been sent me by Harvey Witherspoon Phillips of Tampa, Fla., who says that several other descendants are now living in Europe and Chile. All of them have been invited to attend the unveiling of the statue of their ancestor.

There is another statue of John Witherspoon in Fairmount Park, Philadelphia; Witherspoon Hall is one of the principal buildings at Princeton University, of which he was president, and Witherspoon building in Philadelphia was named in his honor.

---

### Died 115 Years Ago

John Witherspoon died at his country residence, Tusculum, Somerset county, N. J., November 15, 1794, at the age of seventy-two years. His will, probated November 28, 1794, made his wife Anne, whom he married in 1791, sole executrix, and he bequeathed to each of his three children by his first wife, John Witherspoon, David Witherspoon and Ann Witherspoon, $50; to his grandsons, John W. Smith, John W. Ramsay and John Nash Witherspoon, when they reached the age of eighteen years, $250 each; to his daughter, Frances E. Witherspoon, he left half of the residue, and the remainder to his wife, Anne Dill Witherspoon. By a codicil dated November 11, 1794, he left "my daughter Mary Witherspoon, one-half of the estate instead of Frances." It is probable that Frances died in the meantime.

John Witherspoon was married twice. His first wife was Elizabeth, daughter of Robert Montgomery of Carighouse, Ayrshire, Scotland, and two years after her death, in 1789, he married Anne, a young woman of twenty years, widow of Dr. Dill of New York.

By his first wife he had three sons and two daughters; by his second wife, in his old age after his seventieth birthday, he had two daughters.

James Witherspoon, the oldest son, was a major in the revolutionary army, and did good service. He graduated from Princeton College in 1770, and from the little we know of him it may be judged that he was a man of courage and ability. While he was with the northern army, after the battle of Ticonderoga, he was sent with one companion upon a scouting expedition into Canada, and narrowly escaped capture by the British. Finding hiding places in the forest, they succeeded in eluding their pursuers, but were without food, except one biscuit each, for three days, and when they reached headquarters were very nearly exhausted.

James Witherspoon was killed at the battle of Germantown October 4, 1777, by the same cannon ball that killed Gen. Francis Nash of North Carolina, a brigade commander.

---

### Captured by the British

John Witherspoon, the second, graduated from Princeton in 1773, studied medicine, and became a surgeon in the continental army, serving from 1776 until the close of the war. He was sent to Europe to purchase surgical instruments and medicinal supplies, and was on his way on the privateer Degraff when it was captured by the British at St. Eustatius and taken to London as a prisoner. Because of his father's prominence the son was treated with unusual harshness in prison, and John Witherspoon wrote to Benjamin Franklin, then in Paris, to do the best that could be done in his behalf. Franklin finally obtained his release after much difficulty, brought him to Paris and gave him a home until his father sent him money for his expenses back to America. In November, 1779, Franklin was able to start the young physician on his homeward way, and wrote John Witherspoon as follows:

"I hope you will have the pleasure of receiving with this your long-absent son, who appears to me a valuable young man."

President Witherspoon does not seem to have had so high an opinion of his son and namesake, or at least they did not get along very well together. The reason for their differences are not known. Witherspoon left very few family letters and nearly all of those that are preserved were written to his third son, David, who was a school teacher in Virginia, and a member of the faculty of Hampden Sydney College in that state. He also served as "secretary to the president of Congress," but whether that was John Hancock, Peyton Randolph, Henry Middleton, Henry Laurens, John Jay or some other president is uncertain.

In his letters to David President Witherspoon shows a deep paternal affection and solicitude for his son, and his admonitions were admirable. With the students at Princeton he was congenial and considerate, but for some reason or another he quarreled with his namesake and had no communication with him during the later years of John's life which were spent at St. Stephens, S. C., where his cousin, Capt. James Witherspoon, was settled as early as 1734. John practiced medicine there until 1795 when he was lost at sea.

### Career of His Favorite Son

David, the third son, who seems to have been the favorite of his father, graduated at Princeton in 1774, taught school, became a professor at Hampden-Sydney College, Virginia, and was lieutenant of a company made up of students of that college, organized to participate in the battle of Yorktown. He married the widow of Gov. Abner Nash of North Carolina, through whom he inherited a large estate. He located at Newbern, N. C., studied law, was admitted to the bar, and practiced there for several years. The federal census in 1790 shows him to be the largest slave holder in Craven county, N. C., owning 113 slaves. After the death of his wife, in 1799, he returned to reside at Princeton, where he died and was buried in 1801. He left one son, John Nash Witherspoon, D. D., LL. D., who graduated at the university of North Carolina, was admitted to the bar, afterward studied theology and was pastor of Presbyterian churches at Raleigh, N. C. and Columbia, S. C. He was the ancestor of a large number of the most prominent members of the present generation.

Anne, the oldest child of John Witherspoon, married the Rev. Dr. Samuel Stanhope Smith, who, with his brother, John Blair Smith, founded Hampden-Sydney College, Va., and succeeded his father-in-law as president of Princeton College, in the year 1794. Her daughter Frances married Joseph Cabell Breckinridge, father of John Cabell Breckinridge, of Kentucky, member of the House of Representatives, United States senator, Vice President of the United States, major general and secretary of war under the Confederacy.

Another of Anne's daughters married John W. Smith, a judge of New Orleans, from whom a number of prominent people have descended. Another daughter married James Provost, stepson of Aaron Burr, and had two sons and two daughters, all of whom married well.

Frances, the second child and second daughter of President Witherspoon, married Dr. David Ramsey, who was educated at Princeton, while her father was president and became eminent as a surgeon, a historian, a politician, a philanthropist and a leader of public opinion, first in New Jersey and afterward in South Carolina, where he afterward resided during the greater part of his life. He was captured at the siege of Charleston in 1780, imprisoned by Cornwallis in St. Augustine, Fla., for eleven months, was elected to Congress, was president pro tem of the United States Senate and served for several years in the House of Representatives. He wrote a history of South Carolina, a history of the Congregational Church, a history of the United States, a "Universal History Americanized" in twelve volumes, a life of George Washington and a dozen or more other volumes, and at the same time was active in politics and practicing medicine in Charleston. His industry was intense, and it is related that he slept only four hours a night and spent the remainder of the time between sunset and sunrise in literary labor. He was distinguished for his oratorical powers as well as his literary ability. His second wife was the daughter of Henry Laurens of revolutionary fame.

Mary Ann was one of two daughters born to John Witherspoon by his second wife after he was seventy years of age. The other daughter died in infancy. Mary Ann married Rev. James S. Woods, D.D., of Mercersburg, Pa., and became the mother of Lieut. Woods of the United States Navy, who distinguished himself in the Mexican war and was killed at the battle of Monterey. Her grandson, Rev. David Walker Woods, is the biographer of John Witherspoon, and will pronounce the benediction at the unveiling of the monument.

### Name Spelled Many Ways

The name is variously spelled Weytherspoon, Wethirspoon, Witherspone, Wotherspone, Wotherspoon, and it appears in other forms. Alexander Wotherspon, an uncle of President Witherspoon, was one of the most eminent merchants in Glasgow, and acquired a large fortune. Four brothers signed the call to the Rev. John Hamilton to become pastor of the Barony Church of Glasgow, as follows:

"John Witherspoon,
"William Wotherspon,
"Alex Wotherspon,
"James Witherspon."

Several members of the family came to the United States long before the Rev. John Witherspoon was called to the presidency of Princeton College. Another John Witherspoon, an uncle of the signer, emigrated as early as 1734, accompanied by his family, which seems to have been numerous. Others came in 1736 and settled in the same state. Robert, a brother of John, the signer, came over early, and settled at Yadkin, N. C. Another brother lived near Kingstree, S. C., while James, a cousin, born at Williamsburg, S. C., in 1750, was a captain in the war of the revolution under Gen. Marion, who admired his courage, bravery and ability, and wrote him the following letter:

"St. Stephens, April 4, 1782.
"Sir: Inclosed is a commission to command the Kingstree Company, and hope you will accept it. In these times, when our country calls for men of bravery and ability, such as you, no good man, well wisher to his country, ought to resign or shun the service, and it gives me great pleasure to see you at the head of a company, which I know will be eye witness of whom I have always had the highest opinion and the greatest regard for. I am, sir, your obedient servant. FRANCIS MARION."

Several of John Witherspoon's descendants have occupied seats in the United States Senate and House of Representatives, several have been presidents of colleges and judges of our courts, many have served in the army and the navy, but more have occupied pulpits in Presbyterian churches.
