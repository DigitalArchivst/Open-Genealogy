# 100 Skills in 100 Days

A daily practice guide for genealogists learning to work with AI — from first conversation to autonomous agents. One skill per day, ten phases, each building on the last.

Designed by a council of five experts. Grounded in the [Open-Genealogy](https://github.com/DigitalArchivst/Open-Genealogy) toolkit and aligned with the [CRAIGEN](https://craigen.org/) Guiding Principles for responsible AI use in genealogy.

**No programming required through Day 88.** Days 89–96 introduce the command line. Days 97–100 introduce autonomous agents.

## Design Principles

The council agreed on six principles before drafting a single skill:

1. **One day, one skill.** Each skill should take 15–45 minutes. Some will take less. A few — especially in later phases — may spill into a second sitting. That's fine.

2. **"One day" means "the day you tackle it."** This is not a calendar commitment. Skip weekends. Take a week off. The number is a count, not a deadline.

3. **CRAIGEN principles are woven throughout, not bolted on.** Privacy, accuracy, disclosure, education, and compliance appear as recurring threads, with dedicated skills at key moments.

4. **Every skill is concrete and observable.** Not "understand context windows" but "open a long conversation, scroll to the top, and notice when the AI has forgotten your first instruction."

5. **Open-Genealogy tools are the lab.** Where a skill can be practiced with a specific repository tool, it names the tool. The repo is the hands-on component.

6. **The last skill is the most important.** Day 100 is "teach someone else." If you can teach it, you own it.

---

## How to Use This Guide

- **Start at Day 1** unless you're confident you've already mastered a phase. Skim the phase summary — if everything looks familiar, skip ahead.
- **Each skill has three parts:** a name, a description of what you'll do, and (where applicable) an Open-Genealogy tool to practice with.
- **Checkpoint days** (every 10th day) consolidate what you've learned. Don't skip them — they're where skills become habits.
- **Platform-agnostic** unless labeled otherwise. Most skills work across ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, and other capable models.
- **CRAIGEN-aligned skills** are marked with **[CRAIGEN]** and reference one of the five principles: Accuracy, Disclosure, Privacy, Education, or Compliance.

## Phase 1: First Contact (Days 1–10)

*Your first conversations with AI. No prior experience assumed.*

| Day | Skill | What You'll Do |
|-----|-------|----------------|
| 1 | **Have a conversation with an AI chatbot** | Open ChatGPT, Claude, or Gemini. Ask a question about your family history. Read the answer. Ask a follow-up. You've just had a multi-turn conversation with AI. |
| 2 | **Ask about something you already know** | Ask the AI about a topic you know well — a historical event, a place you've lived, your profession. Find something it gets wrong. This is the most important skill in the entire list. |
| 3 | **Upload a photograph** | Drag a historical family photo into the chat. Ask "What do you see?" Notice how much — and how little — the AI can extract from a photograph. |
| 4 | **Upload a document** | Attach a PDF, a census page, or a scan of a handwritten letter. Ask the AI to summarize it or pull out names and dates. |
| 5 | **Ask "How did you arrive at that?"** | Instead of accepting an answer, ask the AI to show its reasoning. Learn to distinguish when it's quoting a source versus when it's guessing. |
| 6 | **Compare two AI models** | Ask the same genealogy question to two different models (e.g., ChatGPT and Claude). Notice where the answers agree and where they diverge. Neither is always right. |
| 7 | **Understand what you're sharing [CRAIGEN: Privacy]** | Before your next upload, pause. Know that text and images you send to a chatbot may be stored, reviewed, or used for training. Never paste Social Security numbers, financial accounts, or sensitive identifiers. Be cautious with information about living people. Check the platform's data-use policy. |
| 8 | **Try a voice conversation** | Use ChatGPT's voice mode or a similar feature. Ask a genealogy question out loud. Notice how the conversational style changes what the AI says — and what you ask. |
| 9 | **Ask AI to explain a genealogy concept** | Pick something you've always been fuzzy on: "What's the difference between a primary and secondary source in genealogy?" or "What does 'reasonably exhaustive research' mean?" Compare the answer to a trusted reference later. |
| 10 | **Checkpoint: Take stock** | Write down three things AI does well for genealogy and three things it doesn't. Save this list — you'll revisit it on Day 100. |

## Phase 2: Thinking Critically (Days 11–20)

*Building the habit of evaluating AI output instead of accepting it.*

| Day | Skill | What You'll Do |
|-----|-------|----------------|
| 11 | **Catch a hallucination** | Ask the AI to recommend a specific book about your research area, or cite a specific source for a claim. Check whether the source actually exists. When it doesn't — and sometimes it won't — you've caught a hallucination. |
| 12 | **Distinguish inference from fact** | Ask the AI to analyze a document. In the response, mark every statement that comes directly from the document versus every statement the AI inferred. They should be easy to tell apart. If they're not, the AI is blurring the line. |
| 13 | **Learn what a knowledge cutoff means** | Ask the AI "What is the most recent event you know about?" Understand that AI models are trained on data up to a certain date. Anything after that date is unknown to them — or worse, they'll guess. |
| 14 | **Read the CRAIGEN Guiding Principles [CRAIGEN: Education]** | Visit [craigen.org](https://craigen.org) and read the five principles: Accuracy, Disclosure, Privacy, Education, Compliance. These were written by genealogists for genealogists, and they will frame the rest of this course. |
| 15 | **Practice the Accuracy principle [CRAIGEN: Accuracy]** | Take one AI-generated genealogical claim and verify it against a primary source. Document what you checked and whether it held up. This is what CRAIGEN means by "verify information through other records." |
| 16 | **Practice the Disclosure principle [CRAIGEN: Disclosure]** | Use AI to draft a short piece of genealogical writing. When you share it — even informally — note that AI assisted in creating it. Observe your own comfort level. Disclosure builds trust. |
| 17 | **Compare AI advice to a professional reference** | Ask the AI about evidence classification. Then read the *evidence-terminology.md* reference file in the Open-Genealogy repository. Notice where the AI simplifies, generalizes, or gets the terminology wrong. |
| 18 | **Understand the "confidently wrong" problem** | Ask the AI a question where the answer is genuinely uncertain — a disputed genealogical lineage, an ambiguous record. Notice whether the AI communicates uncertainty or presents one interpretation as definitive. |
| 19 | **Learn what training data is** | Ask the AI "What data were you trained on?" Understand the difference between an AI drawing on its training data (general knowledge) and an AI drawing on documents you've uploaded (your specific sources). This distinction matters enormously in genealogy. |
| 20 | **Checkpoint: Articulate your trust framework** | In your own words, write down: when should I trust AI output? When should I verify? When should I discard it entirely? There's no single right answer — but having *your* answer is the skill. |

**Practice with:** [evidence-terminology.md](research/reference/evidence-terminology.md)

## Phase 3: Prompt Craft (Days 21–32)

*Moving from "asking questions" to "giving effective instructions."*

| Day | Skill | What You'll Do |
|-----|-------|----------------|
| 21 | **Write a specific, structured prompt** | Instead of "Tell me about census records," write "List the information fields on the 1940 U.S. Federal Census and explain what each one tells a genealogist." Notice how specificity transforms output quality. |
| 22 | **Provide context before asking** | Start a conversation with: "I'm researching a German immigrant family in 1880s Ohio. The surname may have been anglicized from the original German." Then ask your question. Context steers the AI toward relevant, calibrated responses. |
| 23 | **Use role assignment** | Begin with: "You are an experienced genealogist specializing in Eastern European Jewish records." Ask a question about name variations. Notice how the role changes tone, vocabulary, and depth. |
| 24 | **Chain three prompts in one session** | In a single conversation: (1) transcribe a document, (2) extract the key facts, (3) ask for research leads based on those facts. Each step builds on the last. |
| 25 | **Discover the context window limit** | Start a conversation, give it detailed instructions, then keep chatting until the conversation is long. Go back to the beginning and ask about something from your first message. When the AI has forgotten — that's the context window limit. |
| 26 | **Request structured output** | Ask the AI to present its answer as a table, a timeline, or a numbered list rather than prose. Structured output is easier to verify and use. |
| 27 | **Write negative constraints** | Add rules to your prompt: "Do not fabricate sources. If you don't know, say so. Use [citation needed] when uncertain." Test whether these constraints actually change behavior. (They do.) |
| 28 | **Read the evidence-terminology reference** | Open [evidence-terminology.md](research/reference/evidence-terminology.md) in the Open-Genealogy repository. Read the 3×3 framework: Sources (Original/Derivative/Authored), Information (Primary/Secondary/Indeterminate), Evidence (Direct/Indirect/Negative). This is the vocabulary the toolkit uses. |
| 29 | **Paste a full system prompt into chat** | Copy the full text of [research-assistant-v8.md](research/research-assistant-v8.md) (GRA v8) into a new conversation. Notice how a 704-line prompt transforms the AI from a general chatbot into a GPS-aligned research assistant. This is the copy-paste workflow from [GETTING-STARTED.md](GETTING-STARTED.md). |
| 30 | **Use compact vs. full prompt versions** | Try the same task with the full GRA v8 (704 lines) and the compact version (147 lines). Notice the tradeoff: the compact version uses less of the AI's memory but loses detailed examples and guardrails. |
| 31 | **Listen to the five-part prompt audio** | If available, listen to the *steve-on-5-part-prompt.mp3* audio in the media/ folder. Understand the architecture behind the prompts you've been pasting. |
| 32 | **Checkpoint: Write a prompt from scratch** | Without copying from the repository, write a prompt that produces structured, GPS-quality output for a genealogical task you care about. Compare it to the repository version. What did you include that they didn't? What did you miss? |

**Practice with:** [research-assistant-v8.md](research/research-assistant-v8.md), [research-assistant-v8-compact.md](research/research-assistant-v8-compact.md), [evidence-terminology.md](research/reference/evidence-terminology.md)

## Phase 4: Document Work (Days 33–44)

*Using AI to read, transcribe, analyze, and restore historical documents and photographs.*

| Day | Skill | What You'll Do |
|-----|-------|----------------|
| 33 | **Transcribe a handwritten document with AI** | Upload a scan of a handwritten historical document. Ask the AI to transcribe it. Compare the result to what you can read yourself. Note where the AI struggles — faded ink, archaic letterforms, unfamiliar abbreviations. |
| 34 | **Learn diplomatic transcription conventions** | Understand the principle: preserve exactly what's on the page. Original spelling, original layout, original errors. Learn the notation: `[illegible]` for unreadable text, `[word?]` for uncertain readings, `[margin: text]` for marginal notes, `{^insertion^}` for text added between lines. |
| 35 | **Use the OCR-HTR v08 prompt** | Paste [ocr-htr-v08.md](transcription/ocr-htr-v08.md) into a new conversation. Upload a document image. Work through the three-phase protocol: contextual setup, document examination, line-by-line transcription with metadata. |
| 36 | **Explore Jewish document transcription** | Read [jewish-transcription-v2.md](transcription/jewish-transcription-v2.md). Even if you don't work with Hebrew/Yiddish documents, understand the challenges: multiple scripts, multiple languages in one document, names that differ between civil and religious records. It illustrates how specialized transcription can get. |
| 37 | **Analyze a historical photograph** | Upload a historical family photograph. Ask the AI to describe everything it sees: people, clothing, setting, approximate era, inscriptions, studio markings. See how much genealogical information a single photograph contains. |
| 38 | **Use the 9-layer image analysis protocol** | Paste [universal-image-analysis-v3.md](image-analysis/universal-image-analysis-v3.md) and upload a photograph. Work through all nine layers — from first impression to lighting analysis to edge markings to genealogical significance. The "Recreation Test" at the end asks: could someone recreate this image from the AI's description alone? |
| 39 | **Examine a cemetery headstone** | Upload a photograph of a headstone. Ask the AI to read every inscription, identify symbols, and estimate dates. If you have a Jewish headstone, this is a preview of Day 40. |
| 40 | **Explore the Hebrew Headstone Helper** | Read [hebrew-headstone-helper-v9.md](hebrew-headstones/hebrew-headstone-helper-v9.md). Learn what gematria dating is — converting Hebrew letter-numbers to dates. Understand the Sunset Rule: Hebrew days begin at sunset, creating systematic one-day discrepancies between Hebrew and Gregorian calendars. Even if you never use this tool, it demonstrates how deep specialized AI prompts can go. |
| 41 | **Restore a damaged photograph** | Upload a damaged historical photo and paste [restoration-v2.md](photo-restoration/restoration-v2.md). Ask the AI to assess and restore damage — scratches, fading, tears. Compare the result to the original. Is the restoration historically appropriate, or does it look anachronistically modern? |
| 42 | **Learn the spectrum: conservation to reconstruction** | Read the descriptions of [photo-conservator-v2.md](photo-restoration/photo-conservator-v2.md) (minimal intervention), [restoration-v2.md](photo-restoration/restoration-v2.md) (balanced), and [photo-reconstructor-v3.md](photo-restoration/photo-reconstructor-v3.md) (aggressive reconstruction). Different situations call for different approaches — there is no single "correct" level of restoration. |
| 43 | **Create an image citation** | Use [image-citation-builder-v2.md](writing-tools/image-citation-builder-v2.md) to generate a standardized citation for a historical photograph or document image. Good metadata now saves hours of confusion later. |
| 44 | **Checkpoint: Process a document end-to-end** | Take one document image and process it through the full pipeline: transcribe (OCR-HTR v08) → extract facts (Fact Extractor v4) → note what research questions it raises. This is Workflow 1 from [GETTING-STARTED.md](GETTING-STARTED.md). |

**Practice with:** [ocr-htr-v08.md](transcription/ocr-htr-v08.md), [universal-image-analysis-v3.md](image-analysis/universal-image-analysis-v3.md), [restoration-v2.md](photo-restoration/restoration-v2.md), [image-citation-builder-v2.md](writing-tools/image-citation-builder-v2.md), [fact-extractor-v4.md](writing-tools/fact-extractor-v4.md)

## Phase 5: GPS Research Methodology (Days 45–56)

*Learning the professional standard for genealogical evidence — the framework that separates research from guessing.*

| Day | Skill | What You'll Do |
|-----|-------|----------------|
| 45 | **Learn the five elements of the Genealogical Proof Standard** | The GPS requires: (1) reasonably exhaustive research, (2) complete and accurate citations, (3) thorough analysis and correlation, (4) resolution of conflicting evidence, and (5) a coherent written conclusion. These five elements underlie every research prompt in the repository. |
| 46 | **Understand the 3×3 evidence framework** | Three layers, each with three categories. Sources: Original, Derivative, Authored. Information: Primary, Secondary, Indeterminate. Evidence: Direct, Indirect, Negative. A single document can contain all three information types simultaneously. This is the core of GPS-based analysis. |
| 47 | **Classify a source** | Take a document you have — a census page, a birth certificate, a family Bible entry. Classify it: Is it an **Original** source (first recording), a **Derivative** (copy, transcription, or database extract), or an **Authored** work (compiled history or genealogy)? |
| 48 | **Classify information within a source** | Using the same document, classify each piece of information. The date of death on a death certificate is **Primary** (from the attending physician). The decedent's birthdate is **Secondary** (from the informant, who wasn't there). Some fields are **Indeterminate** (who provided the occupation?). |
| 49 | **Classify evidence** | Now ask: does this information **directly** answer your research question, **indirectly** imply an answer (requiring inference), or is its absence **negative** evidence (the person doesn't appear in a record where they should)? |
| 50 | **Apply CRAIGEN's Accuracy principle to evidence [CRAIGEN: Accuracy]** | Review your classifications from Days 47–49. Are you sure? What if the "original" source is actually a derivative — a transcription in a database rather than the original register? Accuracy means being honest about what you know and don't know about your sources. |
| 51 | **Use GRA v8 to analyze a real document** | Paste [research-assistant-v8.md](research/research-assistant-v8.md) into a new conversation. Upload a document image. Watch how the AI applies the 3×3 framework, classifies each piece of information, and notes uncertainty. This is the GPS methodology in action. |
| 52 | **Analyze a death certificate** | Upload a death certificate and ask GRA v8 to classify every piece of information. The date of death (primary), the birthdate (secondary), the birthplace (secondary), the parents' names (secondary). One original source, multiple information types. This single exercise makes the 3×3 model click. |
| 53 | **Find conflicting evidence** | Take two sources about the same person that disagree — different birth dates, different birthplaces, different parents' names. Present both to GRA v8 and ask it to evaluate which is more likely correct, and why. |
| 54 | **Resolve a conflict** | Using the conflicting evidence from Day 53, apply preponderance of evidence. Which source is closer to the event? Which informant had firsthand knowledge? Does additional evidence tip the balance? Write a one-paragraph resolution. |
| 55 | **Format a citation** | Take one of your sources and ask the AI to format a citation following *Evidence Explained* conventions. Check the result: does it include who created the source, what it is, when it was created, where it's held, and where within it the specific information appears? |
| 56 | **Checkpoint: Write a mini proof argument** | Pick one research question. Identify three sources. Classify the source, information, and evidence from each. Resolve any conflicts. Write a one-page conclusion stating what the evidence proves, and at what confidence level: proved, probable, possible, not proved, or disproved. |

**Practice with:** [research-assistant-v8.md](research/research-assistant-v8.md), [evidence-terminology.md](research/reference/evidence-terminology.md)

## Phase 6: Writing & Narrative (Days 57–66)

*Transforming raw research into readable, publishable genealogical writing.*

| Day | Skill | What You'll Do |
|-----|-------|----------------|
| 57 | **Extract facts from a document** | Paste [fact-extractor-v4.md](writing-tools/fact-extractor-v4.md) into a conversation. Upload a document. Get back structured LABEL: Value pairs — BIRTH_DATE, LOCATION, PERSON, OCCUPATION. Notice how separating extraction from interpretation forces clarity. |
| 58 | **Turn facts into narrative** | Take the structured facts from Day 57 and paste them into a conversation with [fact-narrator-v4.md](writing-tools/fact-narrator-v4.md). Watch raw data become a paragraph of flowing prose. The pipeline: document → structured data → story. |
| 59 | **Draft a biographical sketch** | Paste [narrative-assistant-v3.md](writing-tools/narrative-assistant-v3.md) and provide raw records — transcriptions, extracted facts, notes. Use Mode A (New Narrative) to produce a GPS-informed biographical sketch with footnotes and a proof summary. |
| 60 | **Revise your own writing** | Take a piece of genealogical writing you've already done — a family history paragraph, a blog post, a research note. Paste it into a conversation with the Narrative Assistant in Mode B (Revision). See what it flags: overclaiming? Missing citations? Unresolved conflicts? |
| 61 | **Profile your writing style** | Paste a sample of your writing into a conversation with [linguistic-profiler-v3.md](writing-tools/linguistic-profiler-v3.md). Get back a profile of your voice: vocabulary patterns, sentence structure, tone, formality level. This profile can be used by other writing tools to match *your* style. |
| 62 | **Get word-choice advice** | Paste [lingua-maven-v9.md](writing-tools/lingua-maven-v9.md) and ask about a genealogical term you're unsure about. Try "relict" vs. "widow," or "illegitimate" vs. "born outside marriage." The usage panel analysis gives you denotation, connotation, register, historical context, and a recommendation. |
| 63 | **Calibrate writing for an audience** | Take the same biographical sketch and ask Lingua Maven to adjust it for three different audiences: a family blog, a genealogical society journal, and a reunion booklet. Notice how vocabulary, tone, and detail level shift. |
| 64 | **Abstract a conversation** | If you have a recorded family interview or oral history transcript, paste it into a conversation with [conversation-abstractor-v2.md](writing-tools/conversation-abstractor-v2.md). Get back a formal abstract: key decisions, facts shared, relationships mentioned, action items. |
| 65 | **Clean up a draft** | Take rough writing and paste it into a conversation with [quick-editor-v3.md](writing-tools/quick-editor-v3.md) or [quick-cleanup-v3.md](writing-tools/quick-cleanup-v3.md). These micro-prompts handle grammar, consistency, and readability without changing your voice. |
| 66 | **Checkpoint: Complete the writing workflow** | Execute the full chain from [GETTING-STARTED.md](GETTING-STARTED.md) Workflow 4: research → profile your voice → draft narrative → polish language (Lingua Maven) → final edit (Quick Editor). Produce one polished, GPS-informed biographical sketch. This is publication-quality work. |

**Practice with:** [fact-extractor-v4.md](writing-tools/fact-extractor-v4.md), [fact-narrator-v4.md](writing-tools/fact-narrator-v4.md), [narrative-assistant-v3.md](writing-tools/narrative-assistant-v3.md), [linguistic-profiler-v3.md](writing-tools/linguistic-profiler-v3.md), [lingua-maven-v9.md](writing-tools/lingua-maven-v9.md), [quick-editor-v3.md](writing-tools/quick-editor-v3.md)

## Phase 7: Custom Assistants & Source-Grounded AI (Days 67–78)

*Building persistent AI tools and learning to anchor AI to your specific sources.*

| Day | Skill | What You'll Do |
|-----|-------|----------------|
| 67 | **Create a Custom GPT** | In ChatGPT, build a Custom GPT with a name, description, and instructions based on an Open-Genealogy prompt. It remembers its role across sessions without re-pasting. *(OpenAI platform)* |
| 68 | **Add knowledge files** | Upload reference documents to your Custom GPT — evidence-terminology.md, a family group sheet, a county history PDF. Now the assistant can draw on your materials in every conversation. *(OpenAI platform)* |
| 69 | **Write anti-fabrication guardrails** | Add rules to your assistant's instructions: "Never fabricate sources or citations. Never invent records. Use [citation needed] when uncertain." Test with questions designed to tempt fabrication. See the gpt-configs/ folder for working examples. |
| 70 | **Build a Gem in Gemini** | Create a persistent assistant in Google Gemini with custom instructions and reference material. Same concept as a Custom GPT, different platform. *(Google platform)* |
| 71 | **Understand grounded vs. ungrounded AI** | The difference between a chatbot drawing on general training data and a tool drawing on *your specific sources*. Know when each is appropriate. General knowledge for context; your sources for evidence. |
| 72 | **Add sources to a NotebookLM notebook** | Upload PDFs, paste URLs, or add Google Docs to a NotebookLM notebook. Ask a question. Notice that every answer cites which of your uploaded documents supports it. This is source-grounded AI. *(Google platform)* |
| 73 | **Cross-reference multiple sources** | Upload three documents about the same family to NotebookLM. Ask "What do these documents say about where the family lived in 1890?" Watch NotebookLM synthesize across sources and cite each one. |
| 74 | **Generate an Audio Overview** | Have NotebookLM create a podcast-style conversation about your uploaded sources. Listen during a commute. Notice connections you missed while reading. *(Google platform)* |
| 75 | **Build a genealogical case file** | Create a NotebookLM notebook for a specific research question. Upload census images, vital records, newspaper clippings, and correspondence. You now have an AI-searchable case file grounded entirely in your evidence. |
| 76 | **Set up a Claude Project** | Create a project in Claude with a custom system prompt and uploaded knowledge files. Every conversation within the project inherits that context automatically. *(Anthropic platform)* |
| 77 | **Review platform data policies [CRAIGEN: Privacy]** | Before uploading sensitive documents to any platform (Custom GPTs, NotebookLM, Claude Projects), review its data retention and training-use policies. For client work or records about living people, understand what the platform does with your files. This is CRAIGEN's Privacy principle in practice. |
| 78 | **Checkpoint: Share your assistant** | Make your Custom GPT, Gem, or Project available to someone else — a fellow genealogist, a society member, a research partner. Ask them to test it. Their feedback will teach you more than another week of solo work. |

**Practice with:** [gpt-configs/](gpt-configs/), [evidence-terminology.md](research/reference/evidence-terminology.md), [research-assistant-v8.md](research/research-assistant-v8.md)

## Phase 8: Advanced Workflows (Days 79–88)

*Chaining tools, multi-session research, benchmarking, and professional-level AI integration.*

| Day | Skill | What You'll Do |
|-----|-------|----------------|
| 79 | **Chain: Transcribe → Extract → Analyze** | Execute [GETTING-STARTED.md](GETTING-STARTED.md) Workflow 1 end-to-end. Start with a handwritten document image. Transcribe (OCR-HTR v08), extract structured facts (Fact Extractor v4), then analyze evidence and get research leads (GRA v8). Three tools, one pipeline. |
| 80 | **Chain: Photograph → Analyze → Restore → Cite** | Execute Workflow 2. Start with a historical photograph. Analyze with the 9-layer protocol (Universal Image Analysis v3), restore if damaged (Restoration v2), then create a standardized citation (Image Citation Builder v2). |
| 81 | **Chain: Audio → Transcribe → Abstract → Extract** | Execute Workflow 3. If you have a recorded family interview, transcribe the audio, create a formal abstract (Conversation Abstractor v2), then transform the transcript into structured resources (Transcript Resource Forge v2). No recording? Use any long conversation as input. |
| 82 | **Chain: Research → Profile → Draft → Polish → Edit** | Execute Workflow 4 — the full writing chain. Research with GRA v8, profile your writing voice (Linguistic Profiler v3), draft a narrative (Narrative Assistant v3), polish language (Lingua Maven v9), final edit (Quick Editor v3). Five tools, one publishable result. |
| 83 | **Conduct multi-session research** | Using a Claude Project or ChatGPT Project, maintain a coherent research thread across multiple sessions. Pick up where you left off without re-explaining your question. Update your project knowledge files as research evolves. |
| 84 | **Organize and curate project files** | Review what's in your project workspace. Remove outdated documents. Add new findings. A well-curated knowledge base produces better AI output than a cluttered one. |
| 85 | **Benchmark AI output with the rubric** | Apply the [genealogical-writing-rubric.md](benchmark/rubrics/genealogical-writing-rubric.md) — the 60-point evaluation framework — to a piece of AI-generated genealogical writing. Score it. Identify the weakest areas. This rubric works on AI output *and* human writing. |
| 86 | **Use AI to explain DNA match results** | Ask a chatbot: "What does a 1,200-centimorgan DNA match likely mean?" or "Help me understand my ethnicity estimate." AI is a useful translator for DNA concepts, even if it can't access your actual DNA data. Be cautious with privacy — never share DNA identifiers in a chatbot. |
| 87 | **Review your platform's terms of service [CRAIGEN: Compliance]** | Read the terms of service for the AI platform you use most. What are your rights? What does the platform claim rights to? Can you use AI-assisted work in publications? For client deliverables? This is CRAIGEN's Compliance principle: know your obligations. |
| 88 | **Checkpoint: Complete a full research project** | From question to published narrative. Identify a research question. Gather sources (with AI help). Analyze evidence using the GPS framework. Resolve conflicts. Write a proof argument. Polish it. This is the culmination of 88 days of skill-building. |

## Phase 9: Developer Tools (Days 89–96)

*The command line, GitHub, and AI that works directly with your files. No prior programming experience required — but the terminal will be new territory for many.*

> **A note from the council:** This phase introduces the command line. If you've never opened a terminal, that's completely normal — most genealogists haven't. These skills will walk you through it step by step. The difficulty jump from Phase 8 to Phase 9 is the steepest in this program. Take your time. There's no penalty for spending two or three days on a single skill here.

| Day | Skill | What You'll Do |
|-----|-------|----------------|

## Phase 10: Agents, Ethics & Teaching (Days 97–100)

*Autonomous AI, the CRAIGEN framework as a whole, and the most important skill of all.*

> **A note from the council:** This final phase covers autonomous agents — AI that acts on your behalf without step-by-step supervision. The power is real and so are the risks. OpenClaw, the leading autonomous agent platform as of early 2026, has had significant security incidents including remote code execution vulnerabilities, marketplace skill poisoning (341 malicious skills discovered), and a 1.5-million-API-key database exposure. Treat any autonomous agent as high-risk software. The CRAIGEN principles — especially Privacy and Compliance — are not optional at this level.

| Day | Skill | What You'll Do |
|-----|-------|----------------|
| 97 | **Understand autonomous AI agents** | Learn what an autonomous agent is: AI that can execute multi-step tasks, access tools, browse the web, manage files, and take actions without your approval at each step. Understand the difference between sandbox mode (limited access) and full system access. Know what OpenClaw is, how it works, and why it has been both celebrated and criticized. |
| 98 | **Apply all five CRAIGEN principles together [CRAIGEN: All]** | Review your work from the past 97 days through the lens of CRAIGEN. **Accuracy:** Are you verifying AI output? **Disclosure:** Are you acknowledging AI use? **Privacy:** Are you protecting sensitive data? **Education:** Have you been learning continuously? **Compliance:** Do you understand your platform's terms? Write a personal responsible-AI statement for your genealogical practice. |
| 99 | **Design a responsible AI workflow** | Design (on paper or in a document) a multi-step AI workflow for a genealogical task you care about. Specify: which tools at each step, what data flows between them, where human review is required, what CRAIGEN principles apply at each stage, and what could go wrong. This is architecture, not execution. |
| 100 | **Teach someone else [CRAIGEN: Education]** | Pick one skill from this list — any skill, any phase — and teach it to someone who hasn't done it before. A family member, a society colleague, a friend. Teaching is the highest form of learning. If you can explain it clearly enough that someone else succeeds, you own the skill. Go back and read what you wrote on Day 10. Compare it to what you know now. |

## Council Design Notes

### Dr. Priya Ramachandran — Learning Science

"The 100-day format works because it leverages two established principles: **habit stacking** (attach the new behavior to an existing routine) and **micro-mastery** (complete one small, satisfying unit per day). The checkpoint days every tenth day are essential — they consolidate procedural memory and give learners a sense of progress. The biggest risk is burnout around Days 30–40, where the novelty has worn off but the advanced skills haven't arrived yet. That's why Phase 4 (Document Work) is designed to feel hands-on and immediately useful — you're working with your own family documents, not doing abstract exercises."

"Day 100 — teaching someone else — is Bloom's taxonomy at its peak. If you can teach it, you've internalized it. This should never be moved or made optional."

### Margaret "Meg" Halloran, CG — Genealogical Education

"My society members range from tech-savvy millennials to retirees who just got their first tablet. This curriculum respects that range by starting genuinely at zero (Day 1: have a conversation) and never assuming prior comfort with technology. The CRAIGEN integration matters because our community has been debating AI ethics since 2023 — having a recognized framework woven into a skills curriculum gives it credibility."

"I pushed for Day 86 (DNA interpretation with AI) because DNA is the elephant in the room. Most genealogists encounter DNA results and feel overwhelmed. AI can translate the jargon even if it can't access your actual data. But the privacy note matters — never share DNA identifiers with a chatbot."

"The one gap I still see: this curriculum is for individual researchers. Genealogical societies, libraries, and archives have different needs — group training, institutional policies, staff workflows. That's a different document, but it should exist."

### Dr. Tobias Grant — AI Ethics & Cultural Heritage

"I insisted that CRAIGEN principles appear in Phase 1, not Phase 10. Day 7 (Privacy) is arguably the second most important skill after Day 2 (spotting errors). Beginners are the most likely to paste sensitive data — Social Security numbers, living persons' addresses, DNA results, adoption records — into chatbots without thinking. Every day that passes before they learn this principle is a day of risk."

"The CRAIGEN Alignment Map shows how the five principles distribute across the curriculum. Education is the easiest — the entire course *is* education. Privacy and Accuracy needed dedicated early placements. Disclosure and Compliance are harder to practice without context, so they appear later when learners have actual AI-assisted work to disclose and platforms to evaluate."

"For Phases 9 and 10: I wrote the security advisory about OpenClaw because the documented incidents are severe and recent. This is not hypothetical risk. But I framed it as 'understand the risk and configure accordingly,' not 'avoid autonomous agents entirely.' The technology is too useful to ignore; the risks are too real to dismiss."

### Jordan Reeves — Content Strategy & Engagement

"Pacing is everything in a 100-day program. The phases are deliberately unequal: Phase 1 has 10 days of very easy wins to build momentum. Phase 3 has 12 days because prompt craft is the foundational skill that everything else builds on. Phases 9 and 10 are shorter because they're designed for people who are already deeply engaged."

"The 'one day = the day you tackle it' principle was non-negotiable for me. Calendar commitments kill self-directed programs. This is a count, not a deadline. Some people will finish in 100 days. Some will take a year. Both are fine."

"I argued against time estimates per skill. Any estimate would be wrong for half the audience. Instead, the introduction says '15–45 minutes' as a general range, and the Phase 9 note explicitly says 'take your time.' Trust the learner to pace themselves."

### Aisha Kimathi — Professional Genealogist & AI Practitioner

"I use AI tools every day in client work. The workflows in Phase 8 — especially the four chains from GETTING-STARTED.md — are real. I run Workflow 4 (research → draft → polish → edit) at least twice a week. The fact that this curriculum has learners executing those same chains by Day 82 means they'll have professional-level workflow competency with six weeks of skills behind them."

"I pushed for every Open-Genealogy tool to appear at least once. The repository has over 30 active prompts, and most users only discover 3–4 of them. This curriculum is also a guided tour of the toolkit. The 'Tools Referenced' table at the end makes that explicit."

"The skill I'm most proud of contributing: Day 52, the death certificate analysis. It's the single best exercise for making the 3×3 model click. One document, multiple information types, all clearly distinguishable. I've used it in workshops and it works every time."

## Notes

- **This is a learning path, not a gatekeeping exercise.** You don't need to master every skill before moving to the next phase. Some people will skip phases based on their needs. A genealogist who just wants better transcription can stop after Phase 4 and have gained tremendous value.

- **The tools will change.** Platform names, features, and interfaces evolve. When this curriculum was written, the leading autonomous agent was OpenClaw. By the time you read this, it may have been succeeded by something else. The underlying skills — clear instructions, source awareness, critical evaluation, workflow design — transfer across whatever comes next.

- **Security scales with capability.** The more autonomy you give an AI, the more carefully you should configure permissions, review outputs, and understand what it's doing. This is especially true in Phases 9 and 10.

- **You don't have to do this alone.** Consider working through this curriculum with a partner, a genealogical society study group, or an online community. Shared learning accelerates individual progress.

- **Day 100 matters.** Teaching someone else is not a feel-good bonus — it's how you discover what you actually understand and what you were just following along with. Do it.
